Hi, I'm Isabella Carlton, a Ph.D. student in Physics
at The University of Utah, where I study
stellar dynamics in dense stellar systems.

I explore how stars trace the dark remnants at the centers of dense stellar systems.

I am currently a Ph.D. student studying the dynamics of stars in Omega Centauri, where I focus on stellar kinematics and dynamical modeling to better understand the system's mass distribution. Through observational data analysis and modeling, my work explores dense stellar systems and the potential presence of black holes.

Previously, I worked at NASA Goddard Space Flight Center with Drs. Kim Weaver and Jenna Cann, studying edge-on Active Galactic Nuclei (AGN). Using multi-wavelength observations from XMM-Newton, Chandra, NuSTAR, and VLT/MUSE, I analyzed AGN- and star formation–driven outflows, applying multi-component spectral modeling to constrain key physical properties of galaxies. Before NASA, I was a full-time research assistant at The College of New Jersey investigating galaxy evolution and the role of AGN in post-starburst systems.

Research Interests

Stellar Dynamics in Dense Stellar Systems

Modeling stellar kinematics in globular clusters and dwarf galaxies to map their mass distributions.

Black Holes in Galactic Nuclei

Searching for intermediate mass black holes at the centers of galaxies and clusters through dynamical modeling of stellar tracers.

Active Galactic Nuclei

Probing accreting supermassive black holes with multi-wavelength X-ray and optical observations and spectral modeling.

AGN- and Starburst-Driven Outflows

Disentangling AGN and star-formation contributions to galaxy emission to study feedback and galaxy evolution.
